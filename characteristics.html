Characteristics of ADHD inattention, hyperactivity, impulsivity

Subtype 1 Predominantly Inattentive

Individuals with this subtype often:

  • Find it difficult to pay close attention to details or often make careless mistakes
  • Experience difficulty sustaining attention
  • Appear as though they are not listening
  • Struggle to follow through with instructions
  • Find difficulty with organization, planning, and prioritizing
  • Avoid or dislike tasks that require sustained mental effort
  • Lose things easily
  • Are easily distracted and lose focus
  • Experience forgetfulness with daily activities

Subtype 2 Predominantly Hyperactive/Impulsive

Individuals with this subtype often:

  • Fidget with hands or feet or squirm in chair
  • Find difficulty with remaining seated for extended periods
  • Run or climb excessively as children; experience extreme restlessness as adults
  • Have difficulty engaging in activities quietly
  • Appear as if driven by a motor (adults often feel inside as if they are driven by a motor)
  • Talk excessively
  • Blurt out answers before questions have been completed
  • Experience difficulty waiting or taking turns
  • Interrupt or intrude upon others

Subtype 3 Combined

These individuals present characteristics of both predominantly inattentive and predominantly hyperactive/impulsive ADHD.

Co-occurring Disorders There's usually more to the story.

Many students with ADHD often have at least one other co-existing condition. Though any disorder can co-exist with ADHD, there are some disorders are more commonly identified as co-occurring with ADHD diagnoses.

These disorders include but are not limited to: anxiety, depression, tic disorders, Tourette syndrome, behavior or conduct disorders, substance abuse, sleep disorders and learning disabilities.

As it is common for children with ADHD to present characteristics of other diagnoses as well, it is important to consider how co-existing diagnoses may create different complexities and challenges for students.
